Data preparation

Since the data is presented in multiple CSV files, for convenience in processing, we will write a function that combines them into a single table. The function takes one argument directory, which is the path to the folder where the necessary files are stored

combine_files <- function(directory){
  df <- data.frame()
  for (file in list.files(path = "Data", pattern = "*.csv", full.names = TRUE)){
    if (endsWith(file, suffix = '.csv')){
      data <- read.csv(file)
      df <- rbind(df, data)
    }
  }
  return(df)
}

Comparison of mollusk diameter with 5 and 15 rings:

From this graph, it is evident that the diameter of mollusks with 15 rings is larger than that of mollusks with 5 rings.

Next, we will test the hypothesis of equality of means between these two groups using the Students t-test. I will not perform a normality check in this case, as with more than 30 observations, the t-test can be applied to data that are not normally distributed (in our case, the number of observations is significantly larger than 30).

The t-statistic was -33.22, and also p-value << 0.05 with the number of observations being 100 and 102 in the samples of mollusks with 5 and 15 rings, respectively. Therefore, we can conclude that there are significant differences in the diameters of mollusks with 5 and 15 rings.

How old is the mussel?

First, lets check if there are significant differences in the age composition between male and female individuals. For this, we will use the Wilcoxon test, as we cannot say that the variable Rings is continuous (it is more of a rank).

## 
##  Wilcoxon rank sum test with continuity correction
## 
## data:  filter(combined_data_without_outliers, Sex == "Male")$Rings and filter(combined_data_without_outliers, Sex == "Female")$Rings
## W = 714623, p-value = 0.0003707
## alternative hypothesis: true location shift is not equal to 0

It is evident that male and female groups significantly differ in age composition. Next, I used the Wilcoxon test again, but this time with a one-sided hypothesis, and it turned out that on average, the age of male individuals is lower than that of females.

Overall, the age of mollusks ranges from 4 to 15 rings. The most represented group consists of individuals with 9 rings.
